Research interests

The study of vulnerability to hazards is a highly interdisciplinary field, and it is supported by developments in multiple fields, such as Earth and climate science, social sciences, engineering and architecture, and population and health sciences. My research focuses on urban heat and falls at the intersection of the following topics and fields:

Meteorology and climate science
How to identify heatwaves and extreme heat conditions that threaten livelihoods before they occur
Population vulnerability
The social and geographical determinants of heat impacts that shape sensitivity to extreme heat, and the population's capacity to adapt
Human physiology and thermoregulation
The biophysical mechanisms that regulate the shedding of excessive heat loads by the human body
Remote sensing and big data
Novel GIS/statistical methods that improve the spatiotemporal resolution of mapping heat and inform real-time heat warning systems
Built environment and urban planning
How buildings and urban infrastructure modify heat exposure and the different levers to reduce heat gain (e.g., passive cooling, urban greening, shading)
Cooling technologies
The use of air conditioning or other techniques to mitigate human exposure, and the implications of their use for energy systems and the environment

Selected projects

AC operation

Understanding the factors that determine when and for how long we run air conditioning when it's hot outside.

We used estimates of AC operation rates for around 140,000 households to examine the correlations between AC use and housing characteristics, demographic factors, and climate data. We also identified potential "cooling gaps" where electricity costs may be a deterrent to AC operation by the most vulnerable, even when they own AC systems. Our aim was to improve our understanding of the differences in true adaptive capacity across the population and, thus, of heat vulnerability.

Reflective coatings

Examining the effect of applying 'cool' coatings in school playgrounds on ground temperature and heat stress

We performed a series of transect measurements using a rolling cart with temperature, radiation, humidity, and wind speed sensors, in 3 different schools in the Los Angeles Unified School District (LAUSD). Our objective was to understand the influence of highly reflective coatings on ground and air temperatures, as well as estimate the effect on the thermal heat stress experienced by children. Study in collaboration with the Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory (LBNL).

Heat vulnerability

Incorporating humid heat metrics and air conditioning ownership into heat vulnerability assessments

Climate change is intensifying humid heat events globally, calling for an integrated framework that considers their impacts on the population. Heat vulnerability can be conceptualized as the intersection of three dimensions: heat exposure, sensitivity, and adaptive capacity. Exposure assessments are often based solely on dry bulb temperature, which does not account for the effect of humidity on the thermoregulatory ability of the human body. In this study, we investigate how incorporating humidity influences estimates of heat vulnerability at different times of day, compared to employing only dry bulb temperature to assess heat exposure. We incorporate census tract-level air conditioning (AC) ownership data as a novel and granular measure of adaptive capacity, and assess heat sensitivity using detailed sociodemographic indicators. We find that dry bulb temperature alone fails to capture some extreme heat events, particularly those occurring at night and in coastal regions in Southern California. Additionally, the magnitude and location of populations identified as vulnerable varies among humid heat metrics, so we argue that the choice of metric is nontrivial and should be carefully considered in research and policy aimed at mitigating heat-related health risks.
